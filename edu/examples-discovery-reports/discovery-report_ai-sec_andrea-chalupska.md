Discovery Report — AI Security & Governance
Client: EuroHealth Insurance AG
Perspective: AI Security & Governance (investment mindset)
Purpose: Decide whether it makes business sense to invest in this initiative — and under what conditions

Executive Summary — would I invest my own money?
Yes — but only if the investment is explicitly about control, not “more AI”.
EuroHealth is past the experimentation phase. AI is already running in production and delivering value. That makes this a low‑risk / high‑leverage situation if the next step is done correctly.
The risk is not whether AI works.
The risk is that AI is spreading faster than EuroHealth’s ability to govern, audit, and explain it — especially under EU AI Act scrutiny.
From an investor’s perspective, this is a classic inflection point:

done right → scalable platform, lower cost base, reduced regulatory risk
done wrong → fragmented tools, board escalation, stalled adoption, sunk cost

This discovery confirms that the only rational investment thesis is AI industrialization: governance, auditability, and operating model first — automation second.

1. The real problem (not the one stated)
What the CIO said

“We need to reduce helpdesk costs by 30%.”
“We need one AI platform.”
“We must be EU AI Act ready.”

What that actually means
EuroHealth already has AI tools. The reason they reached out is not missing functionality — it is missing control.
AI is currently:

running in IT (ServiceNow + Moveworks),
emerging in HR and Claims,
unregistered,
inconsistently monitored,
and largely unauditable outside ServiceNow.

That combination is financially dangerous in a regulated insurance business.

This is not a chatbot project.
This is an enterprise control problem created by partial AI success.


2. Why this is investable (from a business perspective)
2.1 Proven value already exists
AI has already reduced L1 ticket volume significantly. This de‑risks the investment — we are not betting on unproven technology.
2.2 The next bottleneck is governance, not innovation
The limiting factor is now:

auditability,
ownership,
policy enforcement,
and the ability to stop or explain AI behavior.

These are structural problems, not feature gaps — and they are solvable.
2.3 Governance investment compounds
A governed AI foundation:

enables cost reduction in IT and
becomes reusable for HR, Claims, Finance without repeating the same risk discussion.

This turns a €180K spend into a platform investment, not a one‑off project.

3. Stakeholders that matter (who can make or break ROI)
CIO (Sponsor, board-facing owner)
The CIO’s success is defined by credibility with the board.
He needs to answer three questions clearly:

What AI do we have?
Who controls it?
Can we prove it?

If any of these are fuzzy, the investment fails politically.
CISO (Hard veto)
The CISO is actively escalating AI risk.
If auditability and enforceable controls are missing, the initiative will be blocked or quietly constrained.
IT Operations (Silent risk)
If governance increases operational friction, teams will route around it.
That recreates shadow AI — the exact problem we are trying to solve.
Helpdesk team (Adoption risk)
The team fears replacement.
Without clear human‑override and accountability, they will resist adoption — and metrics will lie.
HR & Claims (Shadow AI owners)
These teams already built AI without approval.
Unless governance scales beyond IT, the risk simply migrates.

4. Key findings that change the approach
Finding 1 — “On‑prem only” is not a detail, it’s a deal‑shaper
All security, audit, and monitoring must work without cloud assumptions.
Any plan that quietly relies on cloud‑native controls is not viable.
Investment implication: validate feasibility early or stop.

Finding 2 — Knowledge quality is a hidden risk multiplier
~30% of the knowledge base is outdated and has no owner.
Bad answers at scale destroy trust faster than no answers.
Investment implication: data ownership is a prerequisite, not a phase‑2 improvement.

Finding 3 — Autonomy must be constrained by design
In a regulated insurer, full autonomy is not acceptable.
AI must operate with:

clear guardrails,
human checkpoints,
and the ability to pause or roll back behavior.

Investment implication: human‑in‑the‑loop is a core requirement, not a safety net.

Finding 4 — EU AI Act readiness creates value, not just cost
Early compliance:

reduces regulatory risk premium,
strengthens board confidence,
and positions EuroHealth as a “safe” AI operator across multiple EU countries.

Investment implication: compliance is a differentiator, not a tax.

5. What must be true for this investment to make sense (Go / No‑Go)
I would invest only if the following are explicitly agreed:


Clear governance authority
Who approves, registers, and can shut down AI — across IT, HR, and Claims.


Board‑level evidence definition
What exactly proves “AI is under control” (logs, traces, approvals), and where it lives.


On‑prem feasibility validated
Monitoring, logging, and audit are technically possible within budget constraints.


Data ownership assigned
Knowledge base has an owner and a minimum quality gate.


Adoption model defined
Frontline teams understand escalation, override, and responsibility — not just automation.


If any of these remain ambiguous, the ROI case collapses.

6. Risks that could destroy value (explicit)


Paper compliance risk
Policies without enforcement satisfy no regulator and no board.


Shadow AI persistence
Governing IT alone while HR/Claims bypass controls invalidates the narrative.


Trust collapse from bad answers
Outdated knowledge leads to fast reputational damage.


Operational workarounds
Over‑engineered controls drive teams underground.


Timeline illusion
“6 months” without phasing and feasibility checks is not a plan.



7. First two weeks — what convinces me to continue investing
Week 1 — remove uncertainty

Align CIO and CISO on what “defensible AI” means in evidence terms.
Inventory all AI systems (IT, HR, Claims).
Confirm constrained autonomy model and human checkpoints.
Validate on‑prem feasibility for audit and monitoring.

Week 2 — turn intent into enforceable scope

Define first set of non‑negotiable Policy‑as‑Code rules.
Design audit trail schema and retention model.
Assign ownership and quality threshold for the knowledge base.
Align helpdesk leadership on operating and escalation model.

If these steps produce clarity, momentum, and trust, further investment is justified.

8. Why this is the right investment now
EuroHealth is not early — and that’s a strength.
AI is already delivering value, and the organization has reached the point where governance determines whether that value multiplies or collapses.
This discovery shows that the problem is well understood, the constraints are explicit, and the upside is real — if the investment is framed as industrialization, not innovation.
That is a bet I would make.
