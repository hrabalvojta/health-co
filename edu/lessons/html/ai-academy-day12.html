1) Fixed pods + deterministic buddy fallback

  • Work in fixed pods of 5-6 learners for the full day block.
  • Buddy review is deterministic: each person reviews the next pod member in circular order.
  • If your reviewer is silent for 3 minutes, switch to the next available pod member immediately (no-wait rule).

2) Cadence for today

  • Sprint A (20 min): capture role-specific findings from the scenario and CIO inputs.
  • Sprint B (20 min): run deterministic peer review and merge only high-signal clarifications.
  • Sprint C (17 min): publish final discovery evidence with owner, timestamp, and open risk.

3) Minimum contribution + transparency rules

  • Each learner submits at least one artifact update and one review comment per session.

  • If blocked for 5+ minutes, raise fallback immediately through mentor workflow; do not wait for plenary rescue.

  • One Teams thread per pod and one shared folder per day (single source of truth).

Pattern to Notice

Every technical question gets redirected to a business answer. And the business answer reveals a deeper problem than you expected: this is not a chatbot project — it's an industrialization engagement. Hans has tools. He doesn't have governance. Your job is to translate business needs into a unified technical platform after the meeting — not during it.

💡
Translation technique: After the meeting, create a two-column table: "What Hans said" | "What this means technically." And "Cut costs by 30%" translates to "Auto-resolve at least 40% of remaining L1 tickets to offset 30% of the €380K annual helpdesk cost."

🔎 Deep Dive: Questions Nobody Asked (But Should Have)

▼

You've seen the five layers. You know what Hans didn't say. But there's one more skill a consulting detective needs: knowing which questions you should have asked — and didn't.

Every client meeting has a set of questions that nobody asks. Not because they're secret — but because they feel uncomfortable, too specific, or "not my department." These are the questions that separate a report the client files away from a report the client acts on.

⚐

The Power Question

Who can kill this project with a single email?

▼
🔍 Why nobody asks it: It feels political. Impolite. You're supposed to treat all stakeholders equally. But projects don't fail because of technology. They fail because someone with power said "no" and nobody saw it coming.
Questions that should have been asked
YOU
"Hans, if this project needs to change something about how Claims or HR operate — who has the authority to approve that?"
YOU
"Has your CISO signed off on the on-prem AI approach, or is that still being discussed?"
YOU
"If Jan's helpdesk team pushes back on adoption — who makes the final call?"
Why it matters
Hans is your sponsor, but he may not be the most powerful person in the room. The CISO has veto power on any AI deployment. HR and Claims built shadow AI without Hans's approval — meaning they have budget autonomy and may resist centralization. Jan can't kill the project outright, but 12 agents who refuse to use the tool will make your KPIs look terrible at the 6-month review.
AI-PM: Map who has budget, veto, and adoption power AI-SEC: CISO alignment is non-negotiable before Day 1
☠

The Graveyard Question

What have you already tried that didn't work?

▼
🔍 Why nobody asks it: Clients don't like admitting failure. Consultants don't like hearing that someone else already tried their idea. But the graveyard of failed initiatives tells you more about the organization than any strategy deck.
Questions that should have been asked
YOU
"Have you tried to consolidate these AI tools before? What happened?"
YOU
"The three IT tools you mentioned — were they purchased separately or was there a platform evaluation at some point?"
YOU
"What happened to the last technology project that required cross-department cooperation here?"
Why it matters
If there was a previous unification attempt that failed, you need to know why. Was it political? Technical? Budget? If departments historically resist centralization, your governance framework needs a different adoption strategy than if this is their first attempt. The graveyard also reveals organizational antibodies — patterns of resistance that will attack your project the same way they attacked the last one.
FDE: Failed infra projects = landmines in the architecture AI-PM: Past failures set expectations for your project
⚙

The Data Question

Where does the truth actually live in this organization?

▼
🔍 Why nobody asks it: Everyone assumes "the data is there." But in every agentic AI project, the moment you need an agent to actually do something — resolve a ticket, answer a question, enforce a policy — you discover that the data is scattered, outdated, duplicated, or locked in someone's head.
Questions that should have been asked
YOU
"You mentioned the knowledge base is 30% outdated. Who owns it? Who updates it? How often?"
YOU
"When your helpdesk agents resolve a ticket today, where do they look for the answer? One system or multiple?"
YOU
"If I asked three different agents to solve the same ticket, would they give the same answer?"
Why it matters
An agentic AI system is only as good as its data foundation. If the knowledge base has no owner, it will still be 30% garbage when you go live. If agents use three different systems, your RAG pipeline needs to integrate all three — triple the scope Hans described. And if three agents give three answers today, your AI will reproduce that inconsistency at scale unless you build a golden dataset first.
AI-DS: No golden dataset = no reliable model AI-DA: "30% outdated" needs sample validation FDE: Multi-source RAG = architecture decision AI-FE: Agents switch systems = UX research needed
★

The Success Question

How will we know this actually worked — and who decides?

▼
🔍 Why nobody asks it: It feels premature. You haven't started yet, why talk about success criteria? But if you don't define what "done" looks like before you start, every stakeholder will define it differently at the end — and at least one of them will say you failed.
Questions that should have been asked
YOU
"When you present results to the board in six months, what numbers do they need to see?"
YOU
"Is 30% cost reduction measured against current headcount, current ticket volume, or current vendor spend?"
YOU
"If the platform is live but only handles 20% of tickets autonomously — is that success or failure?"
Why it matters
"Cut costs by 30%" sounds measurable. It isn't. 30% of what? Measured how? Against which baseline? Verified by whom? If Hans measures against headcount and you measure against ticket volume, you'll deliver a technical success that Hans reports as a failure. Define the metric, the baseline, and the measurement method in Week 1 — not in Month 5.
AI-PM: No baseline = no measurable success AI-DA: Build the measurement dashboard in Week 1 AI-SEC: Compliance is pass/fail, not a gradient
∞

The Day-After Question

What happens when you leave?

▼
🔍 Why nobody asks it: Consultants are paid to deliver, not to leave. Talking about your own departure feels like admitting you're temporary. But a system that only works while the consultant is in the room is not a system — it's a dependency.
Questions that should have been asked
YOU
"After we hand over the platform — who on your team will own it operationally?"
YOU
"Do you have internal ML engineering capability, or will model updates require external support?"
YOU
"When the EU AI Act requirements change — and they will — who updates the governance framework?"
Why it matters
This is where Layer 5 (commercial opportunity) meets reality. If Hans has no internal team to run the platform, your delivery plan must include a Roadmap to Operate phase — training, documentation, runbooks. And if the answer is "we don't have the capability" — that's not a problem. That's a managed services contract. The best consulting detectives don't just solve the case. They make sure the client can handle the next one without calling them — or knows exactly when to call them back.
FDE: Ops handover = architecture requirement AI-PM: "Roadmap to Operate" is Phase 6 in AI Innovation Lab AI-SEC: Governance ownership must be assigned, not assumed AI-DS: Model drift monitoring needs internal owner

Five layers of what Hans didn't say. Five questions nobody asked. Together, they turn a ten-minute meeting into a discovery report that the client reads and thinks: "They understood something about my problem that I hadn't articulated yet." That is the moment you stop being a vendor and start being an advisor.

If it contains what Hans didn't tell you — but needs to hear — you just became his most trusted advisor.

Every consulting team that walks into EuroHealth will hear the same answers. Hans will say "cut costs," "on-prem only," "EU AI Act deadline." If your report just restates those facts, you are interchangeable. The client has no reason to choose Kyndryl over anyone else.

Not through technology demos. Not through slide decks. Through showing that you see what they cannot see — because you are looking from the outside.

Further Reading — Where These Ideas Come From

The “5 Layers of the Unsaid” framework combines established consulting methodologies with AI-specific discovery patterns. No single source covers all of it — but together, these references give you the full picture.

📖 1981
Peter Block — Flawless Consulting
The foundation for Layers 1 & 2

Block introduced the distinction between the “presenting problem” (what the client says) and the “underlying problem” (what actually needs solving). His core argument: technical problems almost always have accompanying organizational problems that determine whether your solution gets implemented or ignored. His rule “you cannot contract with someone who’s out of the room” is exactly why Layer 2 (people problems) matters. If you read one book on consulting discovery, read this one.

📖 1988
Neil Rackham — SPIN Selling
The research behind “implied vs. explicit needs”

Based on analysis of 35,000 sales calls across 23 countries, Rackham proved that in complex B2B engagements, success comes from converting implied needs (client feels a problem but can’t articulate it) into explicit needs (client states what they want). His four question types — Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff — are a systematic method for uncovering what the client didn’t say. Top performers ask 4x more implication questions than average.

📖 1987
Barbara Minto — The Pyramid Principle
How to structure what you found

Developed at McKinsey, Minto’s framework teaches you to think bottom-up but present top-down. Her SCQA model (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer) is how your discovery report should open. Her MECE principle (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) ensures your findings cover everything without overlap. If your report reads like a data dump instead of a story, you need Minto.

📖 2000
Maister, Green & Galford — The Trusted Advisor
Why trust beats technology

Defines the Trust Equation: Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation. The denominator is what kills most consulting relationships — when you focus on selling your solution instead of understanding their problem. Directly relevant to Layer 5: the best way to earn a Phase 2 engagement is to show the client you care about their problem more than your revenue.

📄 2025
McKinsey — Seizing the Agentic AI Advantage
The enterprise landscape you’re operating in

McKinsey’s concept of the “agentic AI mesh” — agent registries, governance frameworks, autonomy controls, and observability layers — is the technical context behind what Hans needs but can’t articulate. This article shows you what the industry is converging toward, so your discovery report can reference where the market is heading, not just where the client is today.

What’s new here: None of these sources were written for agentic AI discovery specifically. Agent Autonomy Levels — Where Does Your Agent Sit?
EuroHealth = L2 Agent

EuroHealth's AI helpdesk is an L2 agent. It auto-resolves L1 tickets within policy guardrails, but humans monitor, override, and handle escalations. This is not L0 (too expensive — defeats the purpose) and never L3 (too risky for a workplace AI under EU AI Act high-risk classification).

💡

Why this matters today: When you ask Hans "What should the AI do on its own?", his answer determines L0/L1/L2. L2 means every role has guardrail work: AI-SEC writes rules, FDE implements enforcement, AI-FE designs overrides, AI-PM scopes auto-resolve vs. escalation.

💡
Pro tip: In your discovery report, explicitly state which autonomy level you are designing for and why. This single sentence shows the client you understand the stakes — and prevents scope creep toward L3 later.
⚠️

On the hackathon (Day 25): You will face a new client brief with safety-critical requirements. The agent will be L1–L2, never L3. When worker safety is at stake, autonomous decisions are unacceptable. Keep this autonomy framing — you will use it again.

Instructor Note — Autonomy Levels Discussion

Spend 3–5 minutes here. Ask the room: "Who can give me an example of an L3 agent that exists today?" (Answer: very few in regulated industries.) Then ask: "Why is EuroHealth L2 and not L1?" Push them to articulate that L1 is too expensive — it defeats the cost-reduction goal because every ticket still requires human approval.

If students struggle, use the driving analogy: L0 = manual transmission (you do everything), L1 = cruise control (you still steer), L2 = lane assist (car handles routine, you override), L3 = full self-driving (no human).

✅ Knowledge Check

  • C) The AI auto-resolves L1 tickets within guardrails. Humans monitor, override, and handle escalations.

Reveal Answer
C) The AI auto-resolves L1 tickets within guardrails. Humans monitor, override, and handle escalations. L2 means the agent acts independently within defined boundaries. It is not L0/L1 (too expensive, defeats cost-reduction purpose) and never L3 (too risky under EU AI Act). The human role shifts from "do everything" to "monitor and intervene when needed."

How a Bad Discovery Report Looks — What the Client Sees Through Immediately

  • "We recommend a modern AI-powered solution." — Empty buzzword. What solution? What architecture? What trade-offs? The client hears: "They haven't done any homework."
  • "We will use state-of-the-art LLM technology." — Which model? On-prem or cloud? What about the on-premises constraint? If you didn't catch that, you didn't read the brief.
  • Copy-pasted requirements from the brief. — The client already knows what they told you. They want to know what you noticed that they didn't say.
  • No risks section. — Every project has risks. If your report has none, you are either naive or hiding something. Both are disqualifying.
  • "Timeline: 6 months (as per brief)." — You just repeated what they said. A good consultant asks: "Is 6 months realistic given the on-prem constraint, data quality issues, and EU AI Act deadline?"
  • Generic stakeholder map. — "CIO = decision maker" is not insight. "CIO is under board pressure to show AI results, while IT Ops lead can quietly sabotage rollout" — that's insight.

Rule of thumb: If your sentence could appear in any discovery report for any client, it's not specific enough. Delete it and write something that only makes sense for EuroHealth.
