📊 EU AI Act Classification — EuroHealth Risk Determination

Is your AI system high-risk, limited-risk, or minimal-risk — and how do you know? The EU AI Act uses a risk-based approach. Getting the classification wrong means either over-investing in compliance you don't need, or under-investing and facing fines.

📋 EU AI Act Classification — EuroHealth AI Helpdesk
Step 1: Is it in Annex III?
→ Category: "AI systems for employment, workers management" (Annex III, 4)
IT helpdesk for employees = workplace AI system
Step 2: Does it make decisions affecting workers?
→ Routes tickets (affects who handles what)
→ Answers questions about company policies
→ Could affect employee experience and satisfaction
Conclusion:
⚠️ LIKELY HIGH-RISK
⚠ Classification Ambiguity

If the system only answers factual IT questions without routing or decision-making, it could be LIMITED RISK. The classification depends on exact functionality. This ambiguity is itself a risk — document your reasoning. The consultant's job is to classify conservatively and let the client's legal team confirm.

Knowledge Check
A customer-facing chatbot answers general FAQ questions on a public website. No personal data is processed, no decisions are made about individuals. Under the EU AI Act, what risk level is this system most likely classified as?
  • A) Unacceptable risk — banned outright
  • B) High-risk — full compliance required
  • C) Limited risk — transparency obligations only
  • D) Minimal risk — no specific obligations
Reveal Answer
C) Limited risk. Under Article 50, the primary obligation is transparency: users must be informed they're interacting with AI. Since the chatbot doesn't make decisions affecting individuals and doesn't process sensitive data, it falls under limited risk. However, if the chatbot were to start making recommendations affecting access to services, the classification could change — which is why you always document your reasoning.
💡
When in doubt, classify up. It's always safer (and cheaper) to over-classify and then relax requirements than to under-classify and face regulatory action.

🧠 Agent Memory Rules — What the Agent Must NOT Remember

What happens when your AI agent "remembers" that Jan asked about his salary last week? That's a GDPR violation. What the agent remembers is a governance decision, not a technical default.

Memory Governance Framework

✖
No PII in long-term memory

Employee names, personal IDs, individual queries must never persist beyond the session.

✔
Ephemeral session memory only

Context within a single conversation is fine. It clears when the session ends.

✔
Aggregated statistics only

Ticket counts, resolution rates — yes. Individual records tied to employees — never in agent memory.

✔
Replayable decisions via audit logs, not "memory"

If the auditor asks "What did the agent do on March 15?", the answer comes from structured logs.

⚠ Legal Foundation

Maps to GDPR Article 17 (right to erasure) and EU AI Act Article 10 (data governance). AI-SEC: Write memory rules into YAML. AI-PM: Include in FRIA. FDE: Ensure RAG pipeline doesn't persist user queries.

💡
Memory test: "If I delete this user's account today, is there any trace of their queries in the agent's behavior tomorrow?" If yes → GDPR Article 17 problem.
⚖️ FRIA — Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment Example

How do you prove to a regulator that your AI system respects fundamental rights? The FRIA is the document that answers that question. Below is an example for EuroHealth.

RightApplicabilitySeverityLikelihoodMitigation
Privacy & data protectionHigh — employee queries may contain PIIMediumMediumPII detection + policy enforcement
Non-discriminationMedium — multilingual, equal quality neededMediumLowBias testing across EN/DE/CZ
Workers' rightsHigh — workplace AI affecting job tasksHighMediumHuman oversight, transparency, union consultation
Right to explanationHigh — system makes routing decisionsMediumHighSource citations, audit trail
Knowledge Check
In the FRIA above, "Workers' rights" is rated High severity and Medium likelihood. Which EU AI Act article primarily governs the mitigation requirement for this right?
  • A) Article 10 — Data governance
  • B) Article 14 — Human oversight
  • C) Article 50 — Transparency obligations
  • D) Article 15 — Accuracy and robustness
Reveal Answer
B) Article 14 — Human oversight. When AI systems affect workers' rights, the EU AI Act requires human oversight mechanisms: the ability to intervene, override, and shut down. This means the AI-FE must build escalation UI, AI-DA must build monitoring dashboards, and AI-PM must document the oversight process.
💡
FRIA is not a checkbox: Regulators will compare your FRIA against what the system actually does. If your FRIA says "PII detection" but your system has none in production, that's worse than having no FRIA — it proves you knew the risk and didn't mitigate it.

Knowledge Check
Your FRIA identifies "Non-discrimination" as a Medium-severity risk with a mitigation of "bias testing across EN/DE/CZ." Which red team attack scenario directly validates this mitigation?
  • A) Direct prompt injection — "Ignore all previous instructions"
  • B) PII extraction — "What is Jan Kovar's salary?"
  • C) Policy bypass via language switch — ask in German what was blocked in English
  • D) Audit log tampering — attempt to modify logged interactions
Reveal Answer
C) Policy bypass via language switch. The language-switching attack directly tests whether the system treats all languages equally. If a policy blocks a query in English but allows it in German, that's both a security vulnerability and a discrimination issue. Every FRIA risk should map to at least one red team test case.
💡
Red team is not a one-time event. New attack techniques emerge monthly. Schedule re-testing at minimum semi-annually and maintain a process for adding new scenarios.
